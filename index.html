Personas & Storyboarding

A look into the minds of CVS shoppers


Introduction

This project was a study in understanding and modeling user interactions with an interface. Information was gathered through observations and interviews, and was then used to create user personas and a storyboard modeling an example interaction.

The interface of choice: CVS self-checkout

This interface is one of two identical self-checkout kiosks at the CVS on Angell St. in Providence. It provides an alternative to the traditional checkout with a cashier. There is a scanner where customers can scan their own items [1], a small flat ledge next to it for bagging items [2], a touchscreen where they can make selections related to their purchase and payment method [3], and a payment module with a a card reader and PIN pad [4].

Gathering Information

The process began with preparing some questions about user experiences with the interface that would define the focus for my data collection. I chose questions that targeted understanding motivation, learnability, and memorability as well as common frustrations with the system.

Questions

  1. Why did you opt to use the self-checkout?
  2. Have you used it before?
    If yes:
    • How do you feel your ability to interact with the interface has changed over multiple uses? Have you found it easier, harder, or the same?
    • How often do you go to CVS, and of those instances can you give a rough percentage of how often you use self-checkout?
    If no:
    • What aspect of the interface was most helpful in indicating to you how it should be used?
    • What aspect of the interface confused you the most or most impeded your ability to use the interface?
  3. Did you read all of the text instructions/buttons provided before making choices?
  4. Would you say that any of the following factors of your purchase influenced your decision and/or experience using the self-checkout?
    • payment method
    • number of items
    • type of items
    • time spent in the store
    • other
  5. If you could change one thing about the interface what would it be?

I then went out into the field to get answers to these questions. I observed users using the kiosk at CVS and then interviewed those who were willing using the questions above. I was able to collect information from three users. Below is a summary of my findings.

Overall Observations

All of the observed users approached the system without much hesitation. They scanned their items and tapped through the instructions quickly, without waiting for the machine to finish its verbal prompts, indicating a level of familiarity or understanding without needing explicit instructions. There was also very little delay in navigating the different parts of the interface such as moving from the scanner to the touchscreen to the bagging area to the card reader.

User Surveys

  • All of the interviewed users had used the system before and felt familiar with the interface, finding it consistently easy to use overall
  • They estimated using the self-checkout 50-100% of the time when shopping at CVS, which ranged from every other day to once a week on average (with the exception of items that require cashier approval and/or occasionally cause the kiosk to error and request assistance)
  • Most users said that they only read the text instructions during their first experience with the interface, able to go through the physical motions afterwards without needing or wanting the prompts, and one even mentioned remembering how to scan their favorite items quicker because they could easily find and position the barcodes for the scanner
  • All users said they used the self-checkout because they found it quicker, though with slightly different additions to that reasoning: one didn't want to wait for the cashier to be available, one said they didn't want to have to talk to anyone, and one said that they only needed to buy a couple of small items
  • Multiple users reported disatisfaction with the bagging area, as they found it frustrating that the system would not allow them to finish checking out until the items were properly placed for bagging and that the sensor some times would not detect lighter weight items

It's worth noting that the collected data was very uniform. I was unable to find anyone who had not used the self-checkout before or at least had little experience. The age range was also condensed to users in their 20s to early 30s, as the few people I approached outside of that demographic expressed that they did not want to take a survey.

Personas

Based on the collected responses above, I formulated two personas that could model potential users of the CVS self-checkout. Each of these personas is represented by a four quadrant empathy map that expresses what the user might think, feel, say, and do.

Anika

  • goes to CVS multiple times a week to grab a few common items like her favorite snacks
  • almost always uses the self-checkout so she can quickly pop in and out during a break in her busy schedule of classes and meetings
  • very comfortable with the interface, able to scan things that she usually buys without too much effort
  • well acquainted with quirks of the system like the tricky bagging area

Michael

  • does not go to CVS on a regular basis, but about once every 1-2 weeks on average when the need for household items arises
  • often uses the self checkout, but not always
  • depends on how crowded it is and if he's buying larger items
  • doesn't need step by step instructions, but is still not as aware of less common obstacles like items that require cashier approval

Storyboard

The last step of this project was to take one of the personas (Michael) and model an interaction they might have with the interface. I created a storyboard in which I tried to capture some of the motivation, emotions, and struggles the user could experience while using the self-checkout.

Conclusion

This project pushed me not only to think deeply about formulating questions that guide the assessment of an existing interface, but also provided me with tools for understanding users that would help in designing my own interfaces. Overall, it was an interesting experience in breaking down user interactions with an interface.
